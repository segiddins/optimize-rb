# Samuel Giddins — writing voice notes

Sources skimmed: `blog.segiddins.me` (Nostalgia 2022, This time, it was a compiler bug 2020, A Boring Life 2020, several 2024 Residency Updates) and `traveling.engineer` (Ruby Release Reproducibility 2025, Goals for Binary Gems 2025, 2024 in Review, January 2025 Residency Update). SpeakerDeck profile at `speakerdeck.com/segiddins` (38 decks; titles skimmed — "Funding Ruby Infrastructure as a Non-Profit", "Evolution of Rails within RubyGems.org", "The Challenges of Building a Sigstore Client from Scratch", "A Survey of Recent RubyGems CVEs"). The decks are structural/outline-y; the prose voice lives on the two blogs and is what matters for drafting.

## (a) Stylistic tics

1. **Mid-sentence self-interrupting parenthetical, often undercutting what he just said.** He'll make a statement and then qualify it in parens with a side comment rather than a full footnote. "I write software for a living. At least, I pretend to. Most of the time, my job is building & running (& yes, occasionally fixing) software other people have written."

2. **Em-dash as a narrative aside, typically for a scope-setting clause.** The dash isn't for emphasis; it's for interjecting a shrug. "The ruby repository–like many projects of its scope, complexity, and age–has a complex build setup." Same move appears in: "This is the big action item from last year's RubyGems.org security audit, and figuring out how to ask for the work to be done is important – it will steer both the impact of the work as well as the cost."

3. **Sentence-fragment punchlines after a buildup.** He builds a paragraph with long clauses then lands a single short fragment. "Shocker." "Almost." "Well, today I hit the jackpot." The fragment does the comedic work the long sentences set up.

4. **Scare-quoted jargon the first time a term appears, then used bare.** "the 'version' (version + platform string) is in this new format"; "beating his 'give me all the data' drum"; "pages were almost entirely due to spurts of 5xx responses". The scare quotes signal *I know this phrase is doing work, and I'm winking at it*.

5. **Rhetorical-question-as-transition, sometimes stacked.** "But now? Sure, I completed another sprint at work." / "Why take a day off to ski when I can take meetings from the slopes. Why take an extra day when going to a conference when I can be home in time to open my laptop and call into sprint planning? Seriously, why???" Note the unpunctuated rhetorical question ("But now?") mid-paragraph and the triple question marks when he's ratcheting up.

6. **Comma-spliced list of parallel clauses, no "and" before the last item.** He runs three or four short noun phrases comma-separated to evoke rapid-fire: "Few upgrades. Chaos in airports. Ticket prices doubling verses the baselines I had memorized. Seatmates not wearing masks. Middle seats having people in them." Each a sentence. Also within a sentence: "the jetsetter, the mile-chaser, the guy who flew a bunch and fucking loved it."

7. **Casual profanity as intensifier, sparingly, in personal posts.** "I was a frequent flier, damnit." / "the guy who flew a bunch and fucking loved it." Never in technical posts; appears once or twice in a personal post to signal *I actually mean this*.

8. **Meta-commentary about the writing itself.** He breaks the fourth wall about his own piece: "So, to tie it all into the title that I penned in my mind before opening my laptop, I think I'm nostalgic for a life I had." Also: "It, of course, wasn't, because that bug on its own doesn't warrant a blog post." He tells you why the next paragraph exists.

9. **"Of course," as a discourse marker for the obvious-in-retrospect beat.** "Of course, I need to start off by thanking…" / "Of course, I was going to get to Italy from San Francisco via Toronto, Mumbai, and Geneva." Deployed when the thing is only obvious if you already know him.

## (b) Don'ts

1. **No bullet lists in personal prose.** The technical residency updates use them freely, but the essay-shaped posts (Nostalgia, Boring Life, Compiler Bug) are continuous paragraphs. When drafting long-form in his voice, resist breaking flow into bullets even when the content would support it.

2. **No footnotes, no asterisks, no formal hedging vocabulary.** He doesn't write "arguably", "one might say", "it could be argued", or drop footnote markers. Asides go in parens or em-dashes inline. The register is first-person spoken, not academic.

3. **No generic writerly transitions ("Moreover," "Furthermore," "In conclusion,").** Paragraph breaks do the work. Where a softer connective is needed he uses "Anyways," or "Besides," or just drops in a one-word fragment ("Shocker."). Nothing in his prose sounds like it was copy-edited by a newspaper.

## (c) Pastiche — 150-word template

My cat has opinions about the vacuum cleaner. At least, I assume she does. Most of the time she expresses them by going from asleep-on-the-rug to not-on-the-rug in the span of a single frame, which–like most feline position updates–I mostly miss. The Dyson comes out of the closet and she is simply elsewhere. Gone. Shocker. I used to think this was fear, which would be the sensible read, except that she'll reappear two minutes later on top of the bookshelf, tail up, looking bored, as if to communicate "oh, that thing" with a kind of practiced disdain. But is it really disdain? Or is it just the closest approximation to disdain that a seven-pound animal who sleeps sixteen hours a day can muster before resuming the sleeping? I don't know. Either way, she's staring at me now, and honestly, the rug does look cleaner.
